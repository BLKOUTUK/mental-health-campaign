Voices in Relay

A Pilot Podcast for Mental Health Reparations

Introducing the Proposal Through Collective Conversation

A single pilot episode using an innovative relay interview format where participants rotate between interviewer and interviewee, embodying the co-production values at the heart of the mental health reparations proposal. Each voice both questions and is questioned, creating a democratic space where expertise is collective and transformation is shared.

The Relay Format

The relay format positions participants as both subject and object, both petitioned and petitioner. After a brief introduction, six participants engage in 7-minute segments where each person:

  1. Answers questions from the previous participant (7 minutes)
  2. Transitions to interviewer role to question the next participant
  3. Passes the baton continuing the relay until it returns to the anchor

This structure creates urgency (time pressure demands clarity), democratises expertise (everyone is both teacher and learner), and embodies collective intelligence (understanding builds through conversation, not monologue).

"Without community, there is no liberation. This format makes that visible—we're not individuals speaking to an audience, we're a community thinking together."

Pilot Episode: "Beginning the Conversation"

Duration

45 minutes total (5-minute introduction + 6 × 7-minute segments = 47 minutes including transitions)

Episode Goal

Introduce the mental health reparations proposal through six voices that represent different relationships to the work: lived experience, clinical practice, research, commissioning, community organising, and policy-making. By the end, listeners understand what's being proposed, why it matters, who it's for, and how they can engage.

The Six Participants

1. Anchor Participant: Jamal (Black Gay Man, 28, Birmingham)

Role: Community organiser and mental health advocate with lived experience of mental health crisis and recovery

Why Jamal: Anchors the episode in lived experience while demonstrating leadership and expertise. Opens and closes the relay, creating circular structure.

Key Perspective: "The system wasn't built for us. This proposal is about building what should have always existed."

2. Dr. Amara Thompson (Black Lesbian Psychologist, 45, London)

Role: Clinical psychologist specialising in trauma and minority stress

Why Dr. Thompson: Brings clinical credibility and can translate lived experience into mental health frameworks. Can speak to why culturally-specific approaches work.

Key Perspective: "Generic mental health services aren't failing Black queer men by accident—they were never designed to succeed with us."

3. Professor Michael Adeyemi (Nigerian British Researcher, 52, Manchester)

Role: Professor of Public Health researching LGBTQ+ health inequalities

Why Prof. Adeyemi: Provides evidence base and can contextualise the proposal within broader health policy and research landscapes.

Key Perspective: "We have decades of research showing the problem. This proposal is about finally matching the scale of the solution to the scale of the harm."

4. Sarah Chen (NHS Commissioner, 38, Leeds)

Role: Mental health commissioning lead with responsibility for LGBTQ+ health

Why Sarah: Represents the system side—understands budgets, procurement, service specifications. Can speak to what makes proposals implementable.

Key Perspective: "Commissioners want to fund what works. This proposal gives us a roadmap with evidence, costings, and outcomes we can measure."

5. Marcus Williams (Black Trans Man, 31, Bristol)

Role: Peer support worker and community organiser focused on housing and mental health

Why Marcus: Brings intersectional perspective (trans + Black + working class), represents grassroots organising, speaks to structural determinants.

Key Perspective: "You can't therapy your way out of structural violence. This proposal understands that mental health is housing, employment, safety, and community."

6. Councillor David Okafor (Local Government, 44, London)

Role: Cabinet member for health and social care, Borough of Southwark

Why Cllr Okafor: Represents political will and local implementation. Can speak to how national proposals become local action.

Key Perspective: "This isn't just a mental health proposal—it's economic justice, workforce development, and community power. That's why it's politically viable."

Episode Structure: The Relay in Action

Introduction (5 minutes)

Host: Dr. Rob Berkeley, Director of BLKOUT

Sets context for the proposal, explains the relay format, introduces why this conversation matters now. Brief introductions of all six participants (30 seconds each).

Key Frame: "This isn't a traditional interview because the work isn't traditional. Mental health reparations require collective thinking. So we're going to think together."

Segment 1: Jamal interviewed by Rob (7 minutes)

Jamal answers questions from host Rob

Focus: What is the lived experience that makes this proposal necessary? What does mental health crisis look like for Black queer men?

Sample Questions:

  • Walk us through your first mental health crisis. Where were the systems? What was missing?
  • When you finally found support that worked, what made it different?
  • This proposal talks about "reparations." Why that word?

Segment 2: Dr. Thompson interviewed by Jamal (7 minutes)

Dr. Thompson answers, Jamal asks

Focus: What does the clinical evidence say? Why do Black queer men experience mental health differently?

Sample Questions from Jamal:

  • When I was in crisis, the therapist didn't understand my family, my church, or what it meant to be Black and gay. Is that just my experience?
  • What does the research say about why we're 5 times more likely to need mental health support?
  • Can therapy actually work if the therapist doesn't get your life?

Segment 3: Prof. Adeyemi interviewed by Dr. Thompson (7 minutes)

Prof. Adeyemi answers, Dr. Thompson asks

Focus: What does the research evidence base tell us about scale, intervention types, and outcomes?

Sample Questions from Dr. Thompson:

  • I see individual trauma in my clinic every day. You research population health. What's the bigger picture?
  • This proposal suggests 35,000 Black queer men need support. How did we get that number?
  • We've had smaller projects before. Why hasn't that been enough?

Segment 4: Sarah Chen interviewed by Prof. Adeyemi (7 minutes)

Sarah answers, Prof. Adeyemi asks

Focus: How do good ideas become commissioned services? What makes this proposal implementable?

Sample Questions from Prof. Adeyemi:

  • I write research papers. You write service contracts. What would make you commission this?
  • £10.5 million over 5 years—is that realistic? Too much? Not enough?
  • How would you measure whether this worked?

Segment 5: Marcus interviewed by Sarah (7 minutes)

Marcus answers, Sarah asks

Focus: How does structural inequality shape mental health? What are the connections between housing, employment, safety, and wellbeing?

Sample Questions from Sarah:

  • As a commissioner, I fund therapy. You organise around housing. What am I missing?
  • This proposal includes employment support and peer networks alongside therapy. Why?
  • What does "mental health reparations" mean when you're organising at street level?

Segment 6: Cllr Okafor interviewed by Marcus (7 minutes)

Cllr Okafor answers, Marcus asks

Focus: What's the political case? How does national policy become local action?

Sample Questions from Marcus:

  • Politicians talk about mental health in election campaigns. What happens after?
  • If this money became available, could Southwark actually deliver this? What would it take?
  • What makes a mental health proposal politically viable right now?

Closing: Cllr Okafor questions Jamal (3 minutes)

Relay returns to anchor participant

Focus: Bringing the conversation full circle—what does transformation look like?

Final Question from Cllr Okafor:

  • Jamal, you started this relay. We've heard from clinicians, researchers, commissioners, organisers, and politicians. If this actually happened—if we secured the funding and delivered this proposal—what changes for you personally? For your community?

Jamal's closing: Connects his personal story to collective transformation, articulates both immediate impact (services exist, people survive) and long-term vision (community power, self-determination, liberation).

Production Design

Format Principles

  • Intimacy: Recorded in a single room with all six participants present. They can see each other, react to each other, build on each other's words.
  • Simplicity: No music, no sound effects, minimal editing. The content is compelling; production stays out of the way.
  • Accessibility: Full transcript published alongside audio. British Sign Language interpretation available on video version.
  • Transparency: Light editing only (removing long pauses, technical glitches). Keep the natural flow, including moments of emotion or disagreement.

Technical Specifications

  • Recording: Professional studio (1 day: 4 hours recording + 2 hours setup/breakdown)
  • Equipment: Individual microphones for each participant, professional recording/mixing
  • Post-production: Light editing, mastering, transcription
  • Distribution: All major podcast platforms (Spotify, Apple Podcasts, etc.) + YouTube video + dedicated webpage

Why This Pilot Matters

The relay format isn't just innovative audio—it's the proposal's values made audible. When listeners hear six people thinking together, building on each other's expertise, moving seamlessly between lived experience and policy analysis, they're experiencing what co-production sounds like.

The pilot demonstrates that mental health reparations isn't one person's idea or one organisation's agenda. It's a collective understanding emerging from community knowledge, clinical practice, research evidence, and political possibility. The format makes that visible—or rather, audible.

If the pilot succeeds, it creates a template for ongoing dialogue. The relay can continue, bringing in new voices, exploring specific aspects of implementation, tracking progress as the proposal moves towards funding and delivery. But it starts here: six voices, 45 minutes, a conversation that invites everyone listening to join.
